# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Morrow is for university students and recent graduates, initially framed for a South African audience. They may be browsing between classes on a phone or researching their next move on a laptop. Their core job is to quickly discover internships and graduate opportunities that feel relevant, credible, and made for people at the start of their careers.

## Product Purpose

This first release is a frontend-only homepage shell for a future student career network. It should demonstrate the eventual product experience without authentication, database connections, application handling, or other backend behavior. Success means a visitor immediately understands what Morrow is and feels drawn to browse opportunities.

## Positioning

Morrow makes the first step into working life feel clear, current, and worth getting excited about.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: Browse opportunities.
- Secondary CTA: Create a profile, represented as a future-facing frontend shell.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: “Find what comes next.”
- Belief ladder: Morrow understands early careers; the opportunities feel relevant; exploring them is simple; a future profile will make discovery more personal.
- Proof on hand: No verified testimonials, partner relationships, or platform statistics yet. The homepage must not imply real partnerships or live user data.

## Brand Personality

Ambitious, expressive, and considered. Morrow should give students a sense of forward motion and possibility while feeling more like a premium culture brand than a conventional job board.

## Anti-references

Avoid corporate LinkedIn-blue sameness, generic SaaS card grids, childish campus illustrations, inflated statistics, fake social proof, and close imitation of any reference website. Internshala, WayUp, and Gradlinc inform the information structure; Ocelot Chocolate informs the commitment to art direction, color, imagery, and restraint.

## Design Principles

- Lead with opportunity discovery, not account creation.
- Make early careers feel aspirational without making false promises.
- Use a confident visual identity instead of default job-board conventions.
- Show believable sample product UI so the future platform feels tangible.
- Treat mobile as a primary experience, not a reduced desktop layout.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No project-specific compliance target has been requested. Use semantic HTML, visible keyboard focus, readable contrast, reduced-motion support, and touch-friendly controls as standard frontend practice. Responsive mobile behavior is required.
